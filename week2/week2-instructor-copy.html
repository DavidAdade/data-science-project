Activity 2: What is a CSV File?
10 minutes
CSV = Comma-Separated Values
A simple way to store data in a text file. Think of it like a digital spreadsheet.

Visual Demonstration (5 minutes):

Show on screen what a CSV actually looks like:

Player,Team,Points,Rebounds,Assists LeBron James,Lakers,25.7,7.3,7.3 Steph Curry,Warriors,29.4,5.1,6.3 Kevin Durant,Suns,28.2,6.7,5.0

Explain:

  • First line = column names (headers)
  • Each line after = one player (one row)
  • Commas separate the different stats (columns)
  • Computers can read this super fast!

Activity 2: Selecting Columns
20 minutes

The Problem (3 minutes):

"Our dataset has 25 columns. That's a lot! Sometimes we only want to see a few specific stats."

Solution: We can select just the columns we care about.

Single Column Selection (7 minutes):

# Get just player names players = data['Player'] print(players.head()) # Get just points points = data['PTS'] print(points.head())

Explain:

  • Use square brackets with column name in quotes
  • Returns just that one column
  • Column name must match exactly (case-sensitive!)

Multiple Column Selection (10 minutes):

# Get multiple columns - use double brackets! key_stats = data[['Player', 'Team', 'PTS', 'REB', 'AST']] print(key_stats.head()) # Create a scoring summary scoring = data[['Player', 'PTS', 'FG%', '3P%', 'FT%']] print(scoring.head(10))
The double brackets [[]] confuse students. Explain: outer brackets = selection, inner brackets = list of columns. Practice this multiple times.

Activity 3: Filtering Rows - Finding What You Want
35 minutes

Introduction (5 minutes):

Filtering = Finding rows that meet specific conditions. Like searching for players who score over 20 points per game.

Basic Filtering (10 minutes):

# Find all players who score over 25 PPG high_scorers = data[data['PTS'] > 25] print(high_scorers[['Player', 'Team', 'PTS']]) # Find players on the Lakers lakers = data[data['Team'] == 'LAL'] print(lakers[['Player', 'PTS', 'REB', 'AST']]) # Find players with over 10 rebounds per game rebounders = data[data['REB'] > 10] print(rebounders[['Player', 'Team', 'REB']])

Comparison Operators:

  • > greater than
  • < less than
  • >= greater than or equal
  • <= less than or equal
  • == equal to (note: TWO equals signs!)
  • != not equal to
Common mistake: Using = instead of == for comparison. Emphasize that = assigns a value, == checks if values are equal.

Multiple Conditions (10 minutes):

# Find players who score over 20 AND grab over 8 rebounds all_around = data[(data['PTS'] > 20) & (data['REB'] > 8)] print(all_around[['Player', 'PTS', 'REB']]) # Find players who score over 30 OR assist over 10 elite = data[(data['PTS'] > 30) | (data['AST'] > 10)] print(elite[['Player', 'PTS', 'AST']]) # Find Lakers players who score over 15 lakers_scorers = data[(data['Team'] == 'LAL') & (data['PTS'] > 15)] print(lakers_scorers[['Player', 'PTS']])

Important Symbols:

  • & = AND (both conditions must be true)
  • | = OR (at least one condition must be true)
  • Always use parentheses around each condition!

Guided Practice (10 minutes):

Students work on these challenges:

  1. Find all players who average over 5 assists
  2. Find players on your favorite team
  3. Find players who play over 30 minutes per game
  4. Find players who score over 20 AND assist over 5
  5. Find efficient scorers: over 20 PPG AND shooting over 50% FG
Circulate and help students. This is challenging! Encourage them to try, make mistakes, and debug together.
Activity 4: Sorting Data
15 minutes

Why Sort? (3 minutes):

"We want to see the TOP scorers, the BEST rebounders, the MOST efficient shooters. Sorting helps us rank players!"

Basic Sorting (12 minutes):

# Sort by points (lowest to highest) sorted_pts = data.sort_values('PTS') print(sorted_pts[['Player', 'PTS']].head()) # Sort by points (highest to lowest) top_scorers = data.sort_values('PTS', ascending=False) print(top_scorers[['Player', 'Team', 'PTS']].head(10)) # Top 10 rebounders top_rebounders = data.sort_values('REB', ascending=False) print(top_rebounders[['Player', 'REB']].head(10)) # Best assist leaders top_assists = data.sort_values('AST', ascending=False) print(top_assists[['Player', 'Team', 'AST']].head(10))

Key Points:

  • sort_values() arranges rows by a column
  • ascending=False gives you highest to lowest (best to worst)
  • .head(10) shows top 10
Quick Challenge: "Find the top 5 players in ANY stat you choose. Share who they are!"
Activity 5: Putting It All Together
10 minutes

Complex Query Example:

# Find top 10 scorers who play for Western Conference teams # (simplified - assuming we have conference data) west_teams = ['LAL', 'GSW', 'PHX', 'DEN', 'LAC'] west_players = data[data['Team'].isin(west_teams)] top_west_scorers = west_players.sort_values('PTS', ascending=False) print(top_west_scorers[['Player', 'Team', 'PTS']].head(10))

Break Down the Process:

  1. Filter for Western Conference teams
  2. Sort by points
  3. Take top 10
  4. Display key columns
