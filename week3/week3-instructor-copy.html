Activity 1: Why Advanced Stats Matter
15 minutes

Opening Hook (8 minutes):

Question: "Two players both score 20 points. Player A plays 40 minutes. Player B plays 25 minutes. Who's more efficient?"

The Problem with Basic Stats:

  • Points alone don't tell the full story
  • Need to account for minutes played
  • Need to consider shooting efficiency
  • NBA teams use DOZENS of advanced metrics
Advanced Stats Examples:
  • Points Per Minute: How efficient is a player's scoring?
  • True Shooting %: Accounts for 3-pointers and free throws
  • Usage Rate: How much does a team rely on this player?
  • Plus/Minus: Point differential when player is on court

Real Example (5 minutes):

Show comparison on screen:

  • Player A: 25 PPG, 45% FG, plays 38 minutes
  • Player B: 22 PPG, 52% FG, plays 28 minutes

"Who's better? We need to calculate Points Per Minute to really know!"

  • Player A: 25 √∑ 38 = 0.658 PPM
  • Player B: 22 √∑ 28 = 0.786 PPM ‚Üê More efficient!
This shows students that basic stats can be misleading. Advanced calculations reveal deeper truths about performance.
Activity 2: Math on Entire Columns
20 minutes

The Power of Pandas (5 minutes):

Amazing Feature: You can do math on ENTIRE columns at once. No loops needed!

First Example Together (15 minutes):

# Load our data import pandas as pd data = pd.read_csv("nba_stats.csv") # Look at points and minutes columns print(data[['Player', 'PTS', 'MIN']].head()) # Calculate points per minute for EVERYONE at once! data['PPM'] = data['PTS'] / data['MIN'] # See the new column print(data[['Player', 'PTS', 'MIN', 'PPM']].head())

What Just Happened:

  1. We divided the entire PTS column by the entire MIN column
  2. Pandas did the math for ALL 450 players instantly
  3. We created a NEW column called 'PPM'
  4. Now it's permanently part of our dataset!
Check for Understanding: "How long would it take to calculate PPM for 450 players by hand? How long did it take us with code?" (Answer: Hours vs. 1 second!)

More Examples:

# Rebounds per game data['RPG'] = data['REB'] / data['GP'] # Assists per game data['APG'] = data['AST'] / data['GP'] # Points per shot attempt data['PTS_per_FGA'] = data['PTS'] / data['FGA'] # See all our new stats print(data[['Player', 'PPM', 'RPG', 'APG']].head(10))
Encourage students to think creatively: "What other stats could we calculate? Steals per minute? Blocks per game? Three-pointers per game?"
Activity 3: Creating Advanced Metrics
30 minutes

Efficiency Ratings (12 minutes):

# True Shooting Percentage # Accounts for 2-pointers, 3-pointers, and free throws data['TS%'] = data['PTS'] / (2 * (data['FGA'] + 0.44 * data['FTA'])) # Usage Rate - what % of plays involve this player data['USG%'] = 100 * ((data['FGA'] + 0.44 * data['FTA'] + data['TOV']) / data['MIN']) # Assist to Turnover Ratio data['AST_TO_Ratio'] = data['AST'] / data['TOV']

Explain Each Metric:

True Shooting %:

  • Better than regular FG% because it counts 3-pointers as more valuable
  • Accounts for free throws too
  • NBA teams use this instead of FG%

Usage Rate:

  • How much does the offense run through this player?
  • High usage = team relies on them heavily
  • Helps understand a player's role

Assist/Turnover Ratio:

  • For every turnover, how many assists do they get?
  • Higher = better decision maker
  • Great for evaluating point guards
Some students might struggle with complex formulas. Reassure them that understanding WHAT the metric means is more important than memorizing the formula. Professional analysts have these formulas saved as templates.

Find the Leaders (10 minutes):

# Who has the best True Shooting %? top_efficiency = data.sort_values('TS%', ascending=False) print(top_efficiency[['Player', 'PTS', 'TS%']].head(10)) # Who has the highest usage rate? top_usage = data.sort_values('USG%', ascending=False) print(top_usage[['Player', 'USG%', 'PTS']].head(10)) # Best assist/turnover ratios best_decision_makers = data.sort_values('AST_TO_Ratio', ascending=False) print(best_decision_makers[['Player', 'AST', 'TOV', 'AST_TO_Ratio']].head(10))

Now We Can Ask Better Questions (15 minutes):

Activity 1: Why Visualize?

Show vs. Tell (10 minutes):

Question: "I tell you Steph Curry scored 29, 31, 27, 33, 30, 28, 32 points in 7 games. Can you quickly tell me if he's consistent or streaky?"

Hard to tell from numbers alone!

Now show a line graph of those same numbers.

"NOW can you tell? Way easier, right?"

Why Visualize:
  • Human brain processes images 60,000x faster than text
  • Patterns jump out immediately
  • Easier to communicate findings to others
  • NBA teams show coaches charts, not spreadsheets
  • Every data analyst needs visualization skills

Chart Types Preview (5 minutes):

Show examples of each:

  • Bar Chart: Compare quantities (top scorers)
  • Line Chart: Show trends over time (points per game this season)
  • Scatter Plot: Show relationships (points vs. shooting %)
  • Pie Chart: Show proportions (team's scoring distribution)
Activity 2: Your First Bar Chart
25 minutes

Setup Matplotlib (5 minutes):

# Import the visualization library import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # Load our data import pandas as pd data = pd.read_csv("nba_stats_advanced.csv")
Matplotlib = Python's most popular charting library. It's like having a professional graphics designer in your code!

Create Top 10 Scorers Chart (15 minutes):

# Step 1: Get top 10 scorers top_scorers = data.sort_values('PTS', ascending=False).head(10) # Step 2: Create the bar chart plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6)) plt.bar(top_scorers['Player'], top_scorers['PTS'], color='blue') # Step 3: Add labels and title plt.xlabel('Player', fontsize=12) plt.ylabel('Points Per Game', fontsize=12) plt.title('Top 10 NBA Scorers', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') # Step 4: Rotate x-axis labels so names don't overlap plt.xticks(rotation=45, ha='right') # Step 5: Display the chart plt.tight_layout() plt.show()

Break Down Each Step:

  1. plt.figure(figsize=(12,6)) = Set chart size (width, height)
  2. plt.bar() = Create the bars (x-axis data, y-axis data, color)
  3. plt.xlabel/ylabel/title() = Label your chart
  4. plt.xticks(rotation=45) = Angle the player names
  5. plt.show() = Display the finished chart
This might be the most visually exciting moment of the course so far! Make sure everyone successfully creates their first chart. Take screenshots!

Customize Colors (5 minutes):

# Try different colors plt.bar(top_scorers['Player'], top_scorers['PTS'], color='red') # Use a color per bar colors = ['gold', 'silver', 'brown', 'blue', 'blue', 'blue', 'blue', 'blue', 'blue', 'blue'] plt.bar(top_scorers['Player'], top_scorers['PTS'], color=colors) # Use gradients plt.bar(top_scorers['Player'], top_scorers['PTS'], color='skyblue', edgecolor='darkblue', linewidth=2)
Activity 3: Scatter Plots - Finding Relationships
25 minutes

What are Scatter Plots? (5 minutes):

Scatter Plot: Shows relationship between TWO variables. Each dot = one player. Helps answer: "As X increases, what happens to Y?"

Example Questions:

  • Do players who shoot more score more?
  • Is there a relationship between minutes and points?
  • Do efficient shooters also get more assists?

Create Points vs. Shooting % Scatter Plot (15 minutes):

# Clean data - remove any missing values clean_data = data.dropna(subset=['PTS', 'FG%']) # Create scatter plot plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.scatter(clean_data['FG%'], clean_data['PTS'], alpha=0.6, s=50, color='purple') # Labels and title plt.xlabel('Field Goal Percentage (FG%)', fontsize=12) plt.ylabel('Points Per Game', fontsize=12) plt.title('Scoring vs. Shooting Efficiency', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') # Add grid for easier reading plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3) plt.show()

Reading the Chart:

  • Each dot = one player
  • Position shows their FG% (x) and PPG (y)
  • Dots in upper-right = high scoring + efficient (best!)
  • Dots in lower-left = low scoring + inefficient
  • Is there a pattern? Does better shooting = more points?
Analyze Together: "What pattern do you see? Is there a relationship between shooting percentage and scoring?"

Add Player Labels (5 minutes):

# Label the top scorers on the scatter plot top_5 = clean_data.nlargest(5, 'PTS') plt.scatter(clean_data['FG%'], clean_data['PTS'], alpha=0.5, s=50, color='lightblue') plt.scatter(top_5['FG%'], top_5['PTS'], s=100, color='red', label='Top 5 Scorers') # Add names next to top 5 for idx, row in top_5.iterrows(): plt.text(row['FG%'], row['PTS'], row['Player'], fontsize=9) plt.xlabel('Field Goal %') plt.ylabel('Points Per Game') plt.title('Scoring vs. Efficiency (Top 5 Highlighted)') plt.legend() plt.show()
Activity 4: Comparing Teams
20 minutes

Group Data by Team (15 minutes):

# Calculate average points per team team_avg = data.groupby('Team')['PTS'].mean().sort_values(ascending=False) # Get top 10 scoring teams top_teams = team_avg.head(10) # Create horizontal bar chart plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.barh(top_teams.index, top_teams.values, color='green') plt.xlabel('Average Points Per Game', fontsize=12) plt.ylabel('Team', fontsize=12) plt.title('Top 10 Highest Scoring Teams', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') plt.tight_layout() plt.show()

What's New Here:

  • groupby('Team') = Group all players by their team
  • ['PTS'].mean() = Calculate average points for each team
  • plt.barh() = Horizontal bar chart (easier to read team names)
